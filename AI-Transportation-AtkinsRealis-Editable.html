Chatbot vs. AI Agent

06

AI is shifting from simple conversation to workflow execution.

A chatbot answers individual questions. It is useful for quick summaries, explanations, writing support, translation, and brainstorming. But the user still has to manage the full workflow.

An AI agent works toward a broader goal. It can break the task into steps, use tools, apply assigned skills, check intermediate results, and produce a more complete output.

Example:

Chatbot: “Summarize this resilience guidance document.”

Agent: “Review these State DOT resilience plans, compare risk scoring methods, build a table, draft a memo outline, and flag missing information.”

Tools are what the agent can use: PDFs, spreadsheets, Python, GIS, databases, web search, and document generation.

Skills are reusable playbooks: PDF review, data QA/QC, methodology comparison, dashboard requirements, meeting summary, and slide preparation.

Key takeaway:

A chatbot responds. An agent organizes and executes a workflow.

What AI Is Actually Good At and Not Good At?

10

AI is best at language, structure, pattern recognition, and workflow acceleration

Summarizing long documents

Creating outlines and presentation materials

Writing and debugging code

Transforming messy notes into structured content

Extracting themes from documents

Helping design workflows

Important limitation:

AI does not automatically know what is true

It can sound confident while being wrong

AI is not a replacement for professional judgment

Limitations:

AI may:

  • Generate false but plausible information, including facts, citations, numbers, or policies
  • Miss highly specific local context unless that context is provided
  • Struggle with proprietary or internal data unless securely connected
  • Misread maps, tables, engineering assumptions, or model outputs
  • Produce analysis that sounds reasonable but is technically incorrect
  • Be unreliable as the sole basis for legal, engineering, safety, or funding decisions

Key Message

AI should support professional judgment, not replace it.

Why Does AI Sometimes “Lie”? Can I Trust AI?

11

Why Does AI Sometimes “Lie”? Can We Trust AI?

AI does not usually “lie” in the human sense. It is not trying to deceive the user.

Instead, AI generates the most likely response based on language patterns, available context, and the prompt. This means it can produce answers that sound fluent and confident, even when they are wrong. This is called a hallucination (huh-loo-suh-NAY-shun) .

Common causes:

  • Not enough context
  • Misunderstanding the question
  • Missing tables, maps, figures, footnotes, or appendices
  • Difficulty reading long, scanned, or poorly formatted documents
  • Mixing uploaded information with general training knowledge
  • Producing plausible but unsupported analysis

Better question:

Not just: Can I trust AI?

But: Can I verify the answer from a reliable source?

Key Message

AI is useful for brainstorming, drafting, summarizing, and organizing information.

For engineering, legal, safety, funding, or client-facing decisions, AI output must be checked by a human professional.

Trust AI as an assistant, not as an authority.

RAG and Knowledge Bases
A better way to use AI with technical documents

12

How RAG Works — Simple Explanation

RAG means Retrieval-Augmented Generation.

In simple terms, RAG works in two steps:

Step 1: Retrieve

The system searches the selected documents and finds the most relevant sections.

Step 2: Generate

The AI uses those retrieved sections to generate an answer.

The key idea is that the AI is not just answering from memory. It is answering based on the documents we provide.

Behind the scenes, the system converts text into vectors, which are like “meaning coordinates.” Text with similar meaning is placed closer together, even if the exact words are different.

Simple Example

User asks:

Which DOT plans discuss flooding risk?

The document may use different terms, such as:

  • Flooding
  • Sea level rise
  • Storm surge
  • Coastal inundation
  • Drainage impacts
  • Riverine flooding

RAG can find these related sections because they are semantically similar, not only because they share the exact same keyword.

Key Message

RAG helps AI search the right documents first, then answer based on the most relevant source material.

Common Concerns, Guardrails, and Professional Responsibility

15

Common Concerns, Guardrails, and Professional Responsibility

AI concerns are valid, but they should be managed with guardrails — not ignored.

Common Concerns

  • Will AI replace planners or engineers?
  • Can we trust the answer?
  • Is it allowed for client work?
  • What happens to confidential data?
  • Will it create legal or professional risk?

Practical Guardrails

  • Do not upload confidential client data to unauthorized tools
  • Do not treat AI output as final
  • Verify sources, numbers, citations, assumptions, and policy references
  • Use human review for client deliverables and high-risk decisions
  • Follow company, client, and agency AI policies
  • Document AI-assisted workflows when needed

Balanced Answer

AI can improve productivity, but weak review creates risk.

The risk is not using AI; the risk is using AI casually without controls.

Key Message

AI supports the professional team. It does not replace professional judgment or responsibility.

Government-Client Projects: Rules and Guardrails

16

Case 1 — Federal AI Use Policy: OMB M-25-21

Federal agencies are encouraged to adopt AI, but they must manage it through governance, risk management, transparency, and public trust.

OMB M-25-21 tells agencies to accelerate AI use while maintaining safeguards for privacy, civil rights, civil liberties, and high-impact AI use cases.

Source: White House / OMB, “M-25-21: Accelerating Federal Use of AI through Innovation, Governance, and Public Trust.”

Case 2 — Federal AI Procurement: OMB M-25-22

When federal agencies buy AI systems or AI services, they need to consider performance, risk, competition, vendor lock-in, government data rights, privacy, cybersecurity, and cross-functional review.

This matters to consultants because AI-related tools or deliverables may be treated as part of federal technology acquisition or government data use.

Source: White House / OMB, “M-25-22: Driving Efficient Acquisition of Artificial Intelligence in Government.”

AI is not generally banned in government transportation work, but it is increasingly controlled by agency policy, procurement rules, data protection rules, and human-review requirements.

Case 8 — Risk Management Framework: NIST AI RMF

NIST AI Risk Management Framework is not a transportation-specific regulation, and it is voluntary, but it is a major federal reference framework for managing AI risk. It is useful when explaining why project teams should think in terms of trustworthiness, governance, risk mapping, measurement, and monitoring.

Source: NIST, “AI Risk Management Framework.”

Practical takeaway for consultants

Use only client-approved and company-approved AI tools. Do not upload confidential, sensitive, security-related, PII, or nonpublic client data into public AI systems.

Keep humans in the loop. Verify AI outputs, citations, numbers, assumptions, and sources.

For government-client deliverables, treat AI as drafting, research, QA/QC, and workflow support — not as the final authority.

Case 7 — Other State DOT Example: MnDOT

MnDOT has a clear GenAI standard that applies to employees and third parties working on behalf of MnDOT. It says private, confidential, protected, or otherwise not public data must not be entered into public GenAI tools. It also says GenAI should be supplemental, not a replacement for human judgment, and AI output used for decision-making must be verified for accuracy, relevance, and factuality.

Source: Minnesota DOT, “Generative Artificial Intelligence — Standards.”

My View: How We Should Use AI in Our Industry

25

My View: How We Should Use AI in Our Industry

AI is a workflow multiplier, not a standalone expert.

Transportation work depends on data, assumptions, local context, engineering judgment, and stakeholder coordination. AI can help organize, accelerate, and explain this work, but it should be used carefully, practically, and transparently.

Recommended Next Steps

  • Start with low-risk internal tasks
  • Use approved tools only
  • Verify every important output
  • Build reusable prompt templates and examples
  • Identify repeatable workflows, not just one-time experiments
  • Treat AI as part of quality-controlled project delivery

Final Message

AI will not replace transportation professionals.

But transportation professionals who understand both the technical domain and how to use AI will have a major advantage.
